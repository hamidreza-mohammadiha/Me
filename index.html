GameTheory: The Problem

This chapter introduces the basic concepts and definitions of game theory, such as players, strategies, payoffs, outcomes, and equilibria. It also explains the main types and classifications of games, such as normal form, extensive form, simultaneous, sequential, zero-sum, non-zero-sum, symmetric, asymmetric, and repeated games. It also discusses the main problems and challenges that game theory aims to address, such as how to model rational behavior, how to predict and explain the outcomes of strategic interactions, and how to design and implement mechanisms that achieve desirable social goals.


"Game theory is the name given to the methodology of using mathematical tools to model and analyze situations of interactive decision making. These are situations involving several decision makers (called players) with different goals, in which the decision of each affects the outcome for all the decision makers. This interactivity distinguishes game theory from standard decision theory, which involves a single decision maker, and it is its main focus. Game theory tries to predict the behavior of the players and sometimes also provides decision makers with suggestions regarding ways in which they can achieve their goals. The foundations of game theory were laid down in the book The Theory of Games and Economic Behavior, published in 1944 by the mathematician John von Neumann and the economist Oskar Morgenstern. The theory has been developed extensively since then and today it has applications in a wide range of fields. The applicability of game theory is due to the fact that it is a context-free mathematical toolbox that can be used in any situation of interactive decision making."

Classical Game Theory Problems

Game theory is the study of mathematical models of strategic interaction among rational decision-makers. It is used in economics, political science, and psychology, as well as logic, computer science, and biology. Here are some examples of classical game theory problems that illustrate the main concepts and applications of game theory.

  • The Prisoner’s Dilemma: This is a game that involves two prisoners who are interrogated separately by the police. Each prisoner can either cooperate with the other by remaining silent, or defect by confessing and implicating the other. The payoffs depend on the choices of both prisoners. If both cooperate, they each get a light sentence. If both defect, they each get a harsh sentence. If one cooperates and the other defects, the cooperator gets the harshest sentence and the defector gets the lightest sentence. The dilemma is that, although both prisoners would be better off if they both cooperated, each prisoner has an incentive to defect, regardless of what the other does. This leads to a suboptimal outcome where both defect and get a harsh sentence. The prisoner’s dilemma is often used to model situations where individuals face a trade-off between personal and collective interests, such as in public goods provision, environmental protection, or arms races.

  • The Chicken Game: This is a game that involves two drivers who are heading towards each other on a collision course. Each driver can either swerve or stay straight. The payoffs depend on the choices of both drivers. If both swerve, they each get a small payoff. If both stay straight, they both crash and get a large negative payoff. If one swerves and the other stays straight, the swerver gets a large negative payoff and the straight driver gets a large positive payoff. The game is called chicken because it resembles a situation where two people challenge each other to see who will chicken out first. The game has multiple equilibria, where either driver swerves and the other stays straight. However, there is no dominant strategy for either driver, as each driver prefers to do the opposite of what the other does. The chicken game is often used to model situations where there is a conflict of interest and a potential for escalation, such as in nuclear deterrence, brinkmanship, or bargaining.

  • The Battle of the Sexes: This is a game that involves a couple who want to go out for the evening, but have different preferences over where to go. The husband prefers to go to a football match, while the wife prefers to go to a ballet. The payoffs depend on the choices of both spouses. If they both go to the same place, they each get a positive payoff, but the payoff is higher for the spouse who gets their preferred choice. If they go to different places, they each get a negative payoff. The game has multiple equilibria, where either the husband or the wife gets their preferred choice and the other goes along. However, there is no dominant strategy for either spouse, as each spouse prefers to go to the same place as the other, but also prefers their own choice over the other’s choice. The game is called the battle of the sexes because it resembles a situation where a couple has to coordinate on a common activity, but also has conflicting interests. The game is often used to model situations where there is a need for coordination and communication, such as in team formation, coalition building, or voting.

"We must be absolutely clear on this point before we can proceed. The game is not the experience. The game enables the experience, but it is not the experience. This is a hard concept for some people to grasp. The ancient Zen question addresses this directly: “If a tree falls in the forest, and no one is there to hear it, does it make a sound?” This has been repeated so often that it sounds hackneyed, but it is exactly what we are talking about. If our definition of “sound” is air molecules vibrating, then yes, the tree makes a sound. If our definition of sound is the experience of hearing a sound, then the answer is no, the tree makes no sound when no one is there. As designers, we don’t really care about the tree and how it falls—we care only about the experience of hearing it. The tree is just a means to an end. And if no one is there to hear it, well, we don’t care at all."

GamePlay is a term used to describe the way a game is played. It is the specific way in which players interact with a game, and in particular with video games. GamePlay is the pattern defined through the game rules, connection between player and the game, challenges and overcoming them, plot and player’s connection with it. GamePlay is what makes a game enjoyable and engaging for players. GamePlay can be divided into several types, such as cooperative, competitive, casual, hardcore, etc.

Here are some quotes that relate to GamePlay:

  • “Games are a series of interesting choices.” - Sid Meier: This quote suggests that GamePlay is about making decisions that have meaningful consequences and affect the outcome of the game. A good game should offer the player multiple options and trade-offs, and make them think strategically and creatively.

  • “The game is not a game if you don’t play it.” - Gary Gygax: This quote implies that GamePlay is about actively participating and engaging with the game, rather than passively observing or consuming it. A good game should invite the player to explore, experiment, and express themselves through the game.

  • “Play is the highest form of research.” - Albert Einstein: This quote indicates that GamePlay is about learning and discovering new things through the game, as well as having fun and being curious. A good game should challenge the player to acquire new skills, knowledge, and insights through the game.
